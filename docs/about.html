MathLingua

The Problem

Theorems, definitions, axioms, and conjectures are the tools of mathematics. However, they are recorded in books, papers, and websites spread across the internet, and can be difficult to find.

To find, for example, what theorems are known that describe when a Group is solvable, one can do a keyword search to find articles and papers and, after some digging, find Burnside's theorem.

However, is that the only theorem that describes when a group is solvable? Does Burnside's theorem have alternate forms?

Why does one need to search for articles, related to the theorem, and read through the articles hoping to find the theorem? Why does one need to know that name of the theorem to find it?

The Solution

Wouldn't it be better if there was a central place, open for everyone, everywhere, to search directly for theorems and definitions by not just their name or author but by their content, meaning, and structure.

That is, a place where one doesn't look for an article in the hope of finding the desired theorem/definition, but a place to search for the theorem/definition directly.

If there was a system that aggregated the theorems and definitions of mathematics encoded in a way both computers could understand, the system could allow one to find math results by their content and structure.

It could expand definitions inline in a result to make the result easier to understand. It could find similarities between results. It could have direct links from a theorem/definition, back to the books, articles, and papers that talk about it.

It could make it easier for everyone to find the work mathematicians have done, use the tools they created, and cite their work.

This system is the MathLingua system, and the MathLingua language is the language used to encode mathematics in a form that is easy to read and write by both people and computers.

How You Can Help

The MathLingua system is a community driven effort to populate the system with mathematical statements. By encoding your work, or the work of others in the system, everyone can more easily find the work along with links to the original paper or book. This will help people find each others work so that they can use them, build on them, and cite them.

Visit the Contribute page to contribute to the MathLingua system.

The MathLingua Language

An Example

To understand the MathLingua language, we start with an example of encoding the version of the Heine-Borel theorem that states that a subset of the real numbers is compact if and only if it is closed and bounded. 

Result:
. for: X
  where:
  . 'X \subset \reals'
  then:
  . iff:
    . 'X is \real.compact \set'
    then:
    . 'X is \real.closed \real.bounded \set'
Metadata:
. name = "Heine-Borel Theorem"

The General Structure

This example demonstrates the main syntax of the MathLingua language. First, the syntax for encoding the structure of the theorem is through the use of indentation with groups consisting of sections with names followed by a colon, where each section has zero or more arguments.

That, is the outermost group is: 

Result:
Metadata:

Consisting of the sections Result: and Metadata:. The Result: section has a single argument, indicated by the period (.) and indentation. This argument is the group: 

for:
where:
then:

This group has sections for:, where:, and then:. The only argument for the for: section is the identifier X. The only argument for the where: section is the statement 'X \subset \reals'.

This group specifies that for the given identifiers(s) specified in the for: section, where those identifiers satisfy the conditions in the where: section, then the items in the then: section hold.

Note that argument(s) for a section can appear on the same line of a the section separated by commas (as illustrated in the for: section), or on separated lines with a period and space (`. `) prefix (as illustrated in the where: section).

The then: section has a single element, which is the following group: 

iff:
then:

The iff: section only has a single argument, which is the statement 'X is \real.compact \set', and the then: section only has a single argument which is the statement 'X is \real.closed \real.bounded \set'.

This group specifies that the items in the iff: section all hold if and only if the items in the then: section hold.

The Statement Structure

Note that many of the arguments to section in the above example are statements that are enclosed in single quotes. An example is the statement 'X is \real.closed \real.bounded \set'.

MathLingua is designed to use a indentation type syntax to specify the structure of a mathematical result, while a distinct LaTeX like syntax is used to describe statements inside single quotes.

This statement language is inspired by but not a superset of LaTeX. In particular, commands are specified as starting with a backslash (\) like in LaTeX, but the use of periods (.) show similarities and differences to related commands.

For example, a \real.set and a \complex.set are related but unique and the (.) serves to make fully qualified names to identify a command.

Further, the is keyword is a special keyword used to state that the identifiers on the left side of the is satisfy the items on the right side of the is.

In addition, the is keyword special in that multiple commands on the right side of an is are interpreted as being automatically "glued" to the rightmost command.

That is, saying that the statement 'X is \real.closed \real.bounded \set' is true is the same as saying both 'X is \real.closed.set' and 'X is \real.bounded.set' are true.

This allows one to succinctly describe that an identifier simultaneously multiple conditions similar to how mathematics is typically written.
